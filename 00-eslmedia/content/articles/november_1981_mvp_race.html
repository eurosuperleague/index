MVP Race - November 1981

Three tiers, three very different MVP arguments

The first serious MVP board rewards production, availability, team context, and award momentum, with five candidates named in every tier.

Damon Cross November 1981 MVP race

Stop treating one MVP ladder like it can explain three different tiers. It cannot. Tier 1 has a Magic Johnson argument fighting an Adrian Dantley scoring heater. Tier 2 has Moses Malone turning the race into a property dispute. Tier 3 has Kelly Tripucka putting up numbers loud enough to make the rest of the Conference League speak up or get out of the way.

There is one rule before the arguing starts: you had to play enough. The cutoff is 60 percent of your tier's team games, which means five games in Tier 1, five in Tier 2, and six in Tier 3. No cameo candidates. No decorative stat lines. If you were not on the floor enough, you are not in this column.

Numbers get you invited. Winning, availability, and momentum decide where you sit.
Tier 1 Ballot
Champions League MVP Race
  1. 1Magic Johnson, AFC Richmond
    26.0 PPG | 7.0 RPG | 5.9 APG | 8 games | Player of the Month
    This is the best MVP case in Tier 1 because it has everything: elite production, the strongest team context, full availability, and the award momentum to match. Richmond is 6-2, and Magic is the reason the whole tier has to look upward.
  2. 2Adrian Dantley, Atletico Madrid
    29.8 PPG | .662 TS | 2.0 SPG | 6 games
    Dantley has the cleanest scoring argument in the tier. The problem is not him. The problem is that Atletico is not giving his case the same team force Richmond gives Magic.
  3. 3Larry Bird, AC Milan
    26.1 PPG | 8.7 RPG | 4.3 APG | .529 from three
    Bird does not have the monthly award boost yet, but the all-court profile is too strong to bury. Points, boards, passing, shooting, and a positive team margin keep him in the top three.
  4. 4Artis Gilmore, Crystal Palace
    16.9 PPG | 12.5 RPG | 3.3 BPG | 44.0 MPG
    Gilmore is the defensive and workload monster of this race. The case gets dragged down by Palace's record and minus differential, but the individual burden is enormous.
  5. 5Sidney Moncrief, Real Madrid
    26.8 PPG | 5.6 RPG | .630 TS | +9.3 plus-minus
    Moncrief gets the fifth spot over Mark Aguirre and Kevin McHale because Real Madrid's direction and his efficiency give the argument cleaner balance.

Dantley is the strongest pure statistical case. Magic is the strongest MVP case. That distinction matters. MVP is not a scoring title with nicer stationery.

Tier 2 Ballot
Europa League MVP Race
  1. 1Moses Malone, Benfica
    38.7 PPG | 13.0 RPG | 2.9 BPG | Player of the Month
    This is not close. Moses owns the production, the weekly awards, the monthly award, and the entire gravity of Benfica's top-three push. Everybody else is competing for second until further notice.
  2. 2Steve Johnson, Sheffield United
    25.4 PPG | 9.1 RPG | .674 TS | +13.0 plus-minus
    This is the team-context case. Sheffield has the best Tier 2 form, and Johnson's efficiency is absurd. He is not Moses, but he is the best player on the hottest team.
  3. 3Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, Juventus
    27.7 PPG | 9.5 RPG | 3.5 BPG
    Kareem's numbers are still massive. The reason he is third is simple: Juventus has not turned his production into enough separation.
  4. 4Robert Parish, Marseille
    16.7 PPG | 12.8 RPG | 2.7 BPG | +10.7 plus-minus
    Parish is not the flashiest name here, but Marseille is 6-1 and his defensive spine is a big reason. That matters in an MVP race whether it makes noise or not.
  5. 5Jim Paxson, Chelsea
    22.0 PPG | .558 TS | award winner
    Paxson edges Mike Mitchell and Gus Williams because the award signal gives him a little recent peak on top of the scoring case. Chelsea's record keeps him from climbing higher.

Tier 2 is the easiest ballot at No. 1 and the nastiest ballot from No. 2 through No. 7. Moses has lapped the field. After that, you are arguing team dominance against individual burden.

Tier 3 Ballot
Conference League MVP Race
  1. 1Kelly Tripucka, Barcelona
    37.1 PPG | .640 TS | Player of the Month
    Tripucka leads because he has the points, the efficiency, the award momentum, and the team position. Barcelona owns the one promotion line, and he is the face of that argument.
  2. 2David Thompson, FL Fart
    27.7 PPG | .647 TS | award winner
    Thompson has the best challenger case because FL Fart has the margin profile to scare Barcelona. The efficiency is elite, and the recent award work backs it up.
  3. 3Calvin Natt, Manchester City
    30.5 PPG | 10.1 RPG | 6 games
    Natt is the numbers-need-context candidate. Thirty and ten should scream MVP. Manchester City's rough team file makes it a louder individual season than a cleaner ballot case.
  4. 4World B. Free, Inter Milan
    26.5 PPG | 4.0 RPG | 3.5 APG | .602 TS
    Inter's efficiency keeps Free in the top five. The scoring is strong, the shooting profile works, and the team is close enough to keep the case relevant.
  5. 5Joe Barry Carroll, Paris Saint-Germain
    25.1 PPG | 7.5 RPG | award winner
    Carroll gets the fifth spot over Otis Birdsong, Mike Newlin, and Buck Williams because the award bump and center production give him a sturdier lane.

The biggest riser in the whole article is Steve Johnson, because Sheffield's team surge gives his efficient scoring a stage. The strongest statistical case is Moses Malone, because 38.7 and 13.0 is not a discussion starter; it is a door being kicked open. The player who needs the most team-context mercy is Calvin Natt, because a 30-and-10 line on a struggling Manchester City team is still too good to ignore.

So that is the board. Magic, Moses, Tripucka. Three tiers, three leaders, three different arguments. If the rest of the league wants a different race next month, it can start by making these men uncomfortable.
