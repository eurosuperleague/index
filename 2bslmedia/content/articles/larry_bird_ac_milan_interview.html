Larry Bird Has Notes For AC Milan

The rookie star is already scoring like a franchise pillar, but a 2-4 preseason has left him asking harder questions of the room around him.

Malik Sparks One-On-One Interview · Season 13
161Overall
175Potential
$25MCurrent Salary
128Preseason Points

Larry Bird walked into the room looking like a man who had just scored 23 points in 22 minutes and still wanted to know who left the back door open on defense. That is the funny thing about AC Milan's rookie star right now. The box scores are already loud. The mood is not.

Bird is 21 years old, listed as a 161 overall forward with 175 potential, and already carrying a $25 million salary. Through six AC Milan preseason games, he has produced the kind of stat lines that usually calm a fanbase down: 28 points and 9 rebounds in a win over Bayern Munich, 29 and 9 in the rematch loss, 17 against Crystal Palace, 16 with 5 assists in a win over Atletico Madrid, 15 in another Atletico meeting, and 23 in only 22 minutes against Brighton.

The problem is AC Milan are 2-4. So when Bird sat down, the first question was simple.

Are you having fun yet?
"Winning is fun," Bird said. "So no, not as much as I'd like."

There it is. That is not an answer as much as it is a warning label. Bird is not sulking, and he is not throwing furniture through the preseason. But he is also not pretending that numbers become noble just because they look good in isolation.

The Bayern Split

Against Bayern Munich, AC Milan showed both versions of the story. In the first meeting, Milan won 96-83 while Bird played 42 minutes, scored 28 on 11-for-22 shooting, hit 2 of 3 from deep, grabbed 9 rebounds, handed out 4 assists, and finished plus-17. That looked like the blueprint.

What worked in that first Bayern game?
"We played with force," Bird said. "The ball moved. Guys cut. We didn't stand around watching. When we play that way, everybody gets easier shots."

Then came the rematch. Bird scored 29, went 8-for-18 from the field, made 11 of 14 free throws, grabbed 9 rebounds, and still finished minus-17 in a 113-103 loss. That is the kind of line that makes a player cook dinner and then discover the kitchen is on fire.

How do you score 29 and still walk out frustrated?
"They were more physical," Bird said. "I got to the line and made some shots, but they dictated more of the game than we did. If I'm out there and we're getting beat, I'm part of it."

That last line matters. Bird will not separate himself from the team's problems, but he is also not hiding the problems under the rug and calling it veteran leadership. AC Milan's best player is already speaking like a player who knows the difference between scoring and controlling a game.

The Losses Are Talking

The Crystal Palace loss was the first obvious warning. Bird had 17 points, 4 rebounds, and 2 assists, but shot 6-for-19 and finished minus-19 in a 104-93 defeat. He called it bad rhythm, then immediately widened the lens.

Was that just a bad shooting night?
"I took some shots I can make and missed them," Bird said. "I took a couple I shouldn't have. But we gave up 104. I can shoot better, sure. We still have to make teams work harder than that."

That is how great players complain. They wrap it in accountability so nobody can call it selfish, but the message is still sitting right there in the hallway: guard somebody.

Brighton made the point even louder. AC Milan lost 121-99. Bird played 22 minutes, scored 23 points on 9-for-15 shooting, made all five of his free throws, and still finished minus-22 with five turnovers. A point per minute is nice. Watching the opponent score 121 is not nice. Both things can be true, and Bird sounded like a man who has no interest in choosing only one.

What do you do with a line like 23 points in 22 minutes in a 22-point loss?
"You watch it," Bird said. "You don't hide from it. I had five turnovers. That can't happen. But if they score 121, we're not serious enough defensively. That's not one guy. That's all of us."

"If they score 121, we're not serious enough defensively. That's not one guy. That's all of us."

The Atletico Lesson

The Atletico Madrid games showed the difference between Bird carrying possessions and Bird connecting them. In Milan's 93-79 win, he had 16 points, 4 rebounds, 5 assists, only one turnover, and a plus-19 in 27 minutes. Clean game. Clean result. Everybody gets to breathe.

In the 102-79 loss to Atletico, he scored 15 on 7-for-10 shooting in just 22 minutes, but finished with only 2 rebounds, 1 assist, 3 turnovers, and a minus-4. Efficient scoring, bad night, familiar frustration.

Is that the difference right now, whether the rest of the offense moves around you?
"We're better when I don't have to force the game," Bird said. "If I'm passing and guys are moving, we're harder to guard. If I'm just taking shots because we need something, that's not good enough long term."

There is the whole AC Milan preseason in one sentence. Bird can score. That part is no longer under investigation. The harder question is whether Milan can become organized enough that Bird's scoring does not feel like emergency plumbing.

Do Not Panic, Do Not Ignore It

Near the end of the interview, Bird was asked what AC Milan fans should do with a 2-4 preseason. Panic? Shrug? Start making rotation spreadsheets under fake names?

What do you tell AC Milan fans looking at 2-4?
"It's preseason," Bird said. "Don't panic. But don't ignore it either. We've got things to fix. We know that. I know that."

Do not panic, but do not ignore it. That is what you say when there is smoke coming from the team bus and someone is insisting it is just preseason exhaust.

The good news for Milan is obvious. Bird already looks like the centerpiece. He can score in heavy minutes, score in short bursts, get to the line, rebound, pass, and turn one-on-one answers into quiet pressure on everyone else in the room.

The bad news is just as obvious. He has started counting who is helping him push.
