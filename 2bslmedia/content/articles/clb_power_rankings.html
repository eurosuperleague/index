Champions League Power Rankings: Richmond opens at No. 1

Using the current Champions League standings file for form and the team and player databases for roster weight, the first board starts with AFC Richmond and puts several heavyweights under pressure immediately.

The Champions League standings table is still in its early, strange phase: everyone is effectively living off a six-game sample, and the formal win-loss columns have not caught up yet. But the form line in standings.json is enough to sketch a real board, and when that is paired with the roster strength living in teams.json and players.json, a clear shape appears. AFC Richmond gets the first No. 1 spot, Crystal Palace lands right behind them, and the rest of the tier already looks split between true contenders and brands still trying to justify the crest on the shirt.

The Board
Champions League Through the opening six-game sample
  1. 1AFC Richmond
    Last six: 4-2 | Core: Magic Johnson, Michael Cooper, Brad Davis
    No team in the tier matches Richmond's combination of form and top-end star power. Magic Johnson's 164 overall is the best single number attached to any Champions League roster, and the supporting guard play gives Richmond the cleanest identity in the group.
  2. 2Crystal Palace
    Last six: 4-2 | Core: Artis Gilmore, Darrell Griffith, Reggie Theus
    Palace has the most balanced high-end trio in the tier. Griffith and Theus create enough scoring punch that Gilmore does not have to carry every matchup by himself, and the 4-2 start says the talent is translating quickly.
  3. 3Bayern Munich
    Last six: 4-2 | Core: Bernard King, Tree Rollins, Mike Woodson
    Bayern does not have the same glamour average as Palace or Richmond, but the start matters. Bernard King gives them a closer, Tree Rollins gives them a spine, and the record earns them a top-three launch.
  4. 4Real Madrid
    Last six: 3-3 | Core: Sidney Moncrief, Andrew Toney, Dan Roundfield
    Madrid sits in the talent-rich middle. A dead-even start keeps them out of the first tier, but Moncrief and Toney are too serious to ignore. If this board were only about ceiling, they would have a case for the top three.
  5. 5AC Milan
    Last six: 2-4 | Core: Larry Bird, George Gervin, Julius Erving
    This is the hardest placement on the board. Milan owns the best raw top-three average in the section, but a 2-4 line means the name cachet cannot carry them any higher. Bird, Gervin, and Erving still make them the most dangerous team nobody wants to see in a correction week.
  6. 6Brighton
    Last six: 3-3 | Core: Kiki Vandeweghe, Mark Aguirre, Kyle Macy
    Brighton looks functional, competitive, and short on proof. A neutral record with a solid offensive core keeps them out of the danger zone, but there is not enough form yet to push them into the contender class.
  7. 7Atletico Madrid
    Last six: 3-3 | Core: James Donaldson, Johnny Moore, Adrian Dantley
    Atletico is not broken, but the team profile reads flatter than the clubs above them. Dantley gives them punch, Moore gives them control, and Donaldson gives them size, yet the overall board still feels more sturdy than scary.
  8. 8Manchester United
    Last six: 1-5 | Core: Walter Davis, Jeff Ruland, Marques Johnson
    United is the first genuine alarm on the sheet. The names are respectable, but a 1-5 launch is the worst in the tier by a full margin. Until the form catches up with the roster, they have to wear the bottom spot.
Why Richmond Gets The First Crown

Richmond is not just getting credit for Magic Johnson, though starting any board with the best player in the division certainly helps. The bigger point is fit. Michael Cooper and Brad Davis round out a core that makes sense in every phase of the game, and the 4-2 opening record means the group is already proving more than theory. Crystal Palace is right there and probably owns the sturdier interior anchor in Artis Gilmore, but Richmond has the cleaner top-line case because their best player is also the league's most obvious force multiplier.

The team to monitor most aggressively is AC Milan. A 2-4 opening mark should not be hand-waved away, but neither should a top trio of Larry Bird, George Gervin, and Julius Erving. If Milan strings together even two strong weeks, they will jump half this board. The danger with early rankings is overreacting to a small sample; the danger on the other side is pretending the sample does not exist. Milan sits exactly in that tension.

For now, the biggest takeaway from the Champions League tier is that the line between third and seventh is still movable. Richmond and Palace feel like the healthiest openings. Bayern has earned the right to be taken seriously. Madrid and Milan remain the most likely climbers if pedigree starts matching the sheet. And Manchester United already looks like the team under the brightest pressure. That is enough to build a real first board, and enough to make next week's reshuffle worth waiting for.
