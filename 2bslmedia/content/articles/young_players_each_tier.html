Youth Watch - Three Tiers - Season 13

One Young Player to Watch in Each Tier

Every tier has at least one young player who makes the future feel closer than it looked on opening night, and the interesting part is that none of them need the same thing.

Graham Trent Scouting Notebook · Season 13

Preseason standings can be noisy, but young-player evaluation is usually cleaner. You are not really asking whether a prospect has already arrived. You are asking whether the skill shows up quickly enough, often enough, and in enough different contexts to make you revise the timeline. That is the more interesting question, and the ESL already has a few answers.

Champions League
Tom Chambers, AFC Richmond
20 years old · Forward · 4-2 club
He is not carrying Richmond, which is precisely why he is worth watching. On a team with Magic Johnson and enough adult competence to keep the floor level, Chambers gets to grow without pretending to be the savior.

There is a pleasant cruelty to Richmond’s setup. If Chambers misses a rotation or drifts through a possession, somebody older and better can cover the mess. If he cuts hard, runs the wing, or rises into space before the defense is ready, the game opens around him. That is a useful environment for a young forward because it tests feel before it tests vanity.

He is the sort of prospect scouts like to describe as inevitable once they have seen him twice. The frame is already there, the offensive polish is ahead of schedule, and the club around him is good enough to make every gain visible. Richmond looks like a contender now. Chambers is one reason it may still look like one later.

Europa League
Valerio Sagredo, Chelsea
18 years old · Forward · 5-1 club
Chelsea’s record has done what records always do: it has pulled attention toward the top line. Sagredo is the quieter story, which is often how the better ones begin.

At eighteen, he is not supposed to look comfortable inside a winning rotation this quickly. He is certainly not supposed to do it on a team that already has enough veteran heft to keep the urgency high. But there is value in a teenager who does not need the possession to stop and pose for him before he contributes to it.

Sagredo matters because Chelsea may be building on two clocks at once. The first is obvious and attached to the 5-1 start. The second is subtler and attached to whether an eighteen-year-old forward can grow into something the tier has to plan for by winter. When a good team finds a future piece early, the entire project becomes less fragile.

Conference League
Jeff Lamp, Manchester City
20 years old · Guard · 5-1 club
City has opened fast, but the more lasting detail may be that Lamp already looks like the sort of young scorer who bends a scouting report.

There are prettier prospects on paper, but Lamp has the most useful kind of youth offense: the kind that threatens the defense before the rest of the possession has fully formed. He is twenty, already on a club that sits at 5-1, and that combination tends to create a player worth revisiting every two weeks.

He is not the only reason City owns the first Conference League board. He may, however, be the reason the board feels less temporary than the tier usually does. That is enough for now. In scouting, “watch this again” is often the highest compliment available.
